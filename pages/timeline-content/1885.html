Rising Tensions

Roger Williams University (RWU) was rocked by student-led protests that exposed deep-seated tensions between local Black leadership and northern white missionary control.

Student Protests and Allegations

The conflict began when fifty-six students filed formal charges against the university treasurer, Theodore Edwin Balch, alleging serious misconduct, including financial mismanagement, poor food quality, unfair treatment, and sexual harassment.

Escalation and Closure

When RWU President William Henry Stifler attempted to suppress the dissent with threats of expulsion, over 100 students requested honorable dismissal, effectively forcing the school to shut down by February 1887. Although the American Baptist Home Mission Society (ABHMS) initially dismissed the claims, advocacy from local trustees, several of whom resigned in protest, eventually led to Stifler’s replacement by William Edwin Morang.

Historical Significance

Historians consider this pivotal moment emblematic of the strained relationship between Black southern Baptists and their northern benefactors, demonstrating that students were active agents in demanding racial equity and self-determination within their own institutions.
