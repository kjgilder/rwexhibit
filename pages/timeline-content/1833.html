Early Educational Efforts

Nashville’s first African American educational efforts operated amid intense surveillance and periodic disruption. Founded in 1833 by Alphonso Sumner, a free Black barber, the informal school began with approximately 20 students but grew rapidly despite legal prohibitions against instructing enslaved people.

These early classes were forced to operate intermittently, often closing for months at a time due to devastating outbreaks of cholera and smallpox that swept through the city during this period.

Risk, Resistance, and Consequence

To maintain the school’s survival, Sumner leveraged his professional standing as a barber to elite white citizens, providing a measure of protection for his clandestine educational work. However, by 1835, the school’s success drew increasing scrutiny.

Ultimately, these pressures led to the 1836 accusations that Sumner had assisted a fugitive slave, resulting in his public whipping and forced exile to Cincinnati.
