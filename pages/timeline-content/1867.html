Institutional Organization and Leadership

Classes formally began in August 1867 under the name Nashville Normal and Theological Institute. Z.C. Rush served as principal and oversaw the early development of the school's academic structure, while Rev. Daniel W. Phillips taught theology and continued his earlier work training Black ministers.

The institution operated with support from the American Baptist Home Mission Society, which sponsored several freedmen's schools across the South.

Early Academic Structure

The school offered multiple levels of education that reflected the needs of newly freed communities, including preparatory instruction, a normal school for teacher training, theological education, and early college-level coursework.

Admission requirements were intentionally accessible — requiring students only to read, write, and complete fourth-grade mathematics to enroll. This model allowed individuals with limited previous schooling to enter higher education while continuing to build foundational skills.

Toward a College Curriculum

By 1871, leaders at the Nashville Normal and Theological Institute were already planning a full collegiate curriculum, reflecting the institution's rapid academic growth.

By 1905, the school had produced nearly 500 graduates, many of whom went on to become teachers and ministers across the South.
