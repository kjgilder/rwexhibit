Secret Continuation of Education

After Alphonso Sumner's school was shut down, education did not disappear in Nashville's Black community. Local residents and sympathetic teachers continued organizing lessons quietly, often in private homes and temporary locations.

Education Under Legal Restriction

Before the Civil War, laws throughout the South prohibited teaching enslaved people to read or write. Despite these restrictions, Nashville's free Black community persisted in seeking educational opportunities for children, often relying on informal networks and discreet instruction.

These efforts created an educational foundation that missionary schools would later build upon during Reconstruction.

Union Occupation and New Possibilities

With Union occupation came new possibilities. When Union forces occupied Nashville in 1862, the city became one of the first places in the Confederacy where education for formerly enslaved people could take place more openly.

Federal Control Opens the Door

Federal control allowed missionaries, churches, and aid organizations to begin organizing more formal schools for Black students.

Joseph G. McKee and Early Missionary Schools

On October 13, 1863, the first formal white-led colored school in Nashville opened under the leadership of Joseph G. McKee. The school was supported by the United Presbyterian Church and the Western Freedmen's Aid Commission.

This effort marked a pivotal moment in the transition from informal schooling to organized missionary education.

Education and the Coming of Emancipation

The Emancipation Proclamation of 1863 marked a turning point for African American education across the South. For Black communities, education became central to defining freedom and claiming civil rights.

In the years that followed, hundreds of schools were founded, reflecting a widespread belief that literacy and learning were essential tools for political participation and community leadership.
