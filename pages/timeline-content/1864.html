Founding Vision
Training Leaders for Freedom

Roger Williams University began with a clear purpose: to train ministers and teachers who would serve newly freed Black communities across the South. Education was not just about literacy, but about leadership. Churches and classrooms became spaces where freedom was organized, sustained, and passed forward.

The founders envisioned an institution that would prepare graduates to lead congregations, establish schools, and strengthen Black civic life during Reconstruction.

The Founders

Reverend Randal B. Vandavall

1832–1898
  • Born enslaved at Neely's Bend near Nashville, Vandavall rose to become pastor of the "African Mission" at Spring Street Baptist Church in 1862
  • He later co-founded the institution and served as a trustee, embodying a remarkable journey from enslavement to institutional leadership
  • His life reflects a broader transformation that parallels the evolution of the university itself

Reverend Nelson G. Merry

1824–1884
  • Freed in 1845, Merry became the first ordained Black minister in Nashville in 1853 and grew the First Colored Baptist Church to more than 2,000 members
  • He also helped establish multiple churches across the city

His original congregation eventually divided into three enduring institutions:

  • First Baptist Church, Capitol Hill
  • Spruce Street Baptist Church
  • Mount Olive Missionary Baptist Church

Dr. Daniel W. Phillips

1809–1890
  • A Massachusetts missionary and pastor of Central Baptist Church, Phillips taught classes for Black ministers in his home and worked with educator Z.C. Rush to raise funds
  • Together, they purchased an early property near Fort Gillem for the growing school

Phillips helped connect northern philanthropy with local Black leadership, a partnership that defined the institution's early years.

A Basement Classroom (1865)

In 1865, Reverend Nelson Merry allowed Daniel Phillips to hold classes in the basement of the First Colored Mission. What began as informal theological instruction soon became the foundation for a permanent freedmen's school.

Bureau Act

That same year, Congress created the Freedmen's Bureau on March 3, 1865, within the U.S. War Department. The Bureau oversaw education, labor contracts, and aid for formerly enslaved people, helping make schools like this possible.

A Growing Educational Network

Roger Williams University did not emerge alone. It was part of a rapidly expanding Black educational movement in Nashville:

Central Tennessee College (1865)

Founded in 1865 by the Methodist Episcopal Church and became a cornerstone of Black higher education in Nashville. Over time, it evolved into several important institutions. Its medical department developed into Meharry Medical College, which was formally chartered in 1915. After Central's law school closed in 1912, Kent College of Law continued its legacy. In 1900, Central Tennessee College became Walden University, which operated until its closure in 1925.

Fisk Free Colored School (1866)

Together, these institutions positioned Nashville as a center for Black higher education in the Reconstruction South.
